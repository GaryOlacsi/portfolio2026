01

Federal spending transparency · concept study

USASpending.gov

Trillions of dollars of public spending, technically open but practically unreadable. I reframed the platform around the questions real people bring to it (where did the money go, and who decided) instead of the database it sits on top of.

The solution: high-fidelity redesign

Problem

Open data, closed understanding. Powerful for analysts, opaque for the journalists, grant-seekers, and citizens it is meant to serve.

Approach

Heuristic audit, task-based personas, and an IA rebuilt around guided exploration rather than raw query-building.

Design

High-fidelity flows that lead with plain-language narratives and progressive disclosure into the underlying detail.

Takeaway

Transparency is an interaction problem, not a data problem. Access only counts when comprehension follows.

02

Federal court records · concept study

PACER.gov

The public record of the federal courts, locked behind a dated interface and per-page fees. I redesigned the search and access experience so that finding a case does not require already knowing how the courts file one.

The solution: high-fidelity redesign

Problem

A public archive that assumes legal fluency, with search and pricing that punish anyone outside the profession.

Approach

Expert evaluation plus personas spanning pro se litigants, paralegals, and reporters, then a search model built for each.

Design

Restructured navigation, faceted search, and a cost model surfaced before the click, not after.

Takeaway

For public infrastructure, predictability is a feature. Hidden cost and hidden structure are the same usability failure.

03

Labor & economic statistics · concept study

BLS.gov

The Bureau of Labor Statistics publishes the numbers that move markets and policy, inside a site that buries them. I redesigned the path from headline figure to decision-ready context for non-economists.

The solution: high-fidelity redesign

Problem

Authoritative data, navigation-poor delivery. The hard work of measurement undone by the last mile of presentation.

Approach

Audit and personas (policy analyst, small-business owner, student), then an IA organized by question, not by bureau.

Design

High-fidelity data views that pair each figure with what it means and how to read it, defaulting to plain language.

Takeaway

A statistic is only useful once it is interpretable. Design carries the number the last mile to a decision.

How I work

Systems before screens.

My differentiator is that I treat design as a systems problem before it's a screen problem: I frame the whole system first, research how people do the work, how they think about the work in terms of mental models, and then design for adoption.

／01

Frame the system

Map the whole problem (users, tasks, constraints, and the failure modes that show up under load) before committing to a single screen.

／02

Research the work

Heuristic evaluation, interviews, and task analysis to separate what people say they need from what the work actually demands.

／03

Design for adoption

Architecture, flows, and high-fidelity design built so teams ship it and users keep it, measured by use, not by applause.

Enterprise UX Method

Every screen decision has an upstream answer.

Enterprise screen design is a series of decisions: what screens exist, what each contains, what things are called, how data is filtered, what gets emphasis. None of these are matters of taste. Each has a correct answer that lives in the domain and the users' work. These five research activities are how I retrieve those answers before a single screen is drawn.

Research Signal (Input) Screen Decision (Output)
01

Task Analysis

Decomposes the work into goals, subtasks, decision points, and dependencies; the structure that exists before any software does.

Governs

Workflow and screen boundaries. How many screens, where they divide, what data must stay co-present, and where exception paths and save-and-resume belong.

02

Mental Model Extraction

Surfaces how users internally represent the domain: the entities they think in, the names they use, and the cause-and-effect they assume.

Governs

Conceptual structure and navigation logic. When the system's model matches the user's, the interface becomes predictable; that is what "intuitive" means operationally.

03

Taxonomy

Establishes the controlled vocabulary: what things are called, which categories exist, and how they nest. In enterprise domains, vocabulary is contested territory.

Governs

Labels and findability. Nav items, column headers, filter names, statuses, and search behavior all inherit from the taxonomy; weak taxonomy means "miscellaneous" buckets and failed queries.

04

Taxonomy Facets

Identifies the orthogonal dimensions of each entity (geography, time, type, status) so one data set can serve many retrieval paths.

Governs

Filtering and refinement. The filter panel, advanced search, saved views, and table columns; the answer to "what filters should this have?" is never found at the screen level.

05

Information Hierarchy

Ranks everything a screen could show by frequency, criticality, and task position; grounded in observed behavior, not stakeholder opinion.

Governs

Layout, emphasis, and disclosure. Reading order, visual weight, summary versus detail. A defensible hierarchy is what lets a designer say no; an instrument panel is this principle made visual.

Skip the research and you don't avoid the decisions. You make them by guesswork.

In enterprise software, guesswork compounds into training costs, support load, error rates, and workarounds. Upstream modeling is what makes screens fast: the interface improves because the system behind it finally matches the work.
