01

Project overview

The Bureau of Labor Statistics is the principal federal agency measuring labor market activity, working conditions, price changes, and productivity in the U.S. economy.

Its data, the monthly jobs report, the Consumer Price Index, wage growth, and labor force participation, is cited in news broadcasts, congressional testimony, Federal Reserve policy decisions, and academic research every single day.

The BLS public data portal at bls.gov is the only authoritative source for this data. It is used by an estimated 50+ million people per year, ranging from Federal Reserve economists to high school students writing economics papers. Despite this scale and importance, the data explorer tool has not been meaningfully redesigned since approximately 2019, and is widely described by its users as unusable without prior knowledge of BLS data architecture.

This project is a portfolio redesign conceived to complement NDA-restricted enterprise SaaS work. BLS.gov was selected for its public data access, its direct civic impact, and the richness of design challenges it presents, ranging from time-series chart composition to demographic data visualization to data release management. This is the third case study in a three-part portfolio series (alongside the USASpending.gov and PACER.gov redesigns) intended to demonstrate range across distinct federal data domains.

The central thesis

BLS was built to publish data, not to help people use it. The entire architecture optimises for data integrity and official record-keeping. The result is a platform that treats series IDs as the primary interface, renders charts without benchmarks, generates downloads without metadata, and has no mechanism for users to receive alerts when the data they monitor is updated. The redesign’s goal is to transform a filing cabinet into an analysis tool.

Heuristic audit

I conducted a structured evaluation against Nielsen’s 10 Usability Heuristics across 5 core flows: homepage entry, data series lookup, data explorer and chart builder, geographic explorer, and bulk data download. Severity was scored 0 to 4. I identified 4 critical violations, 5 major violations, and 2 minor violations.

Key insight

H1 (the series ID barrier) affects all 6 user archetypes at high severity, making it the single most democratising fix in the redesign. Replacing the series ID entry point with a plain-language indicator picker is not just a usability improvement; it is the prerequisite for any non-expert user being able to access BLS data at all.

03

User research & personas

I developed 6 personas from a synthesis of journalism workflow research, government economics literature, workforce development practice, and academic data-use studies. They span a wider expertise range than either the USASpending.gov or PACER redesigns: from Federal Reserve-level economists who want vintage data and dual-axis normalised charts, to citizens who need a plain-language benchmark to understand whether 4.1% unemployment is good or bad.

Concerned citizen, one-time user · Low data literacy
Jobs to be done: Understand whether the job market in her industry is improving; verify a statistic from the news.
Pain: H5, H2. Homepage press releases assume econ literacy she lacks; no benchmarks to interpret numbers.
The Jordan / Rashida tension

Jordan (the reporter) needs the headline number instantly and a shareable chart within 18 minutes of release. Rashida (the economist) needs multi-series comparison with vintage data and recession shading for a policy report. These represent the speed-versus-depth tension in the design. The resolution, a Quick mode that pre-configures common single-indicator views and a Builder mode that exposes the full multi-series composition surface, gives both users a first-class experience without either paying a tax for the other.

Information architecture

The IA redesign addresses three structural failures: the series ID prerequisite, the absence of a dedicated release management surface, and the fragmentation of demographic data across disconnected sub-series.

Navigation model: five sections

The five global nav items are organized around user intent.

Four core IA principles

Each encodes a structural decision, not a label change.

Chart Canvas as shared leaf

One composition surface reachable from Explore, every Indicator page, and every Geo profile. Pre-loads the series from wherever the user came from. No series ID required.

Series IDs resolved invisibly

The UI speaks in indicator names. Series IDs are an implementation detail surfaced only in the API reference. No user ever has to type LNS14000000.

Releases as first-class nav

Data release management elevated from a buried PDF to a primary nav section with calendar view, subscribe alerts, and countdown.

Demographics as a filter layer

Breakdowns are a persistent filter on every Indicator page and Chart Canvas, not separate series requiring separate lookups. One chip click, not three series IDs.

The most consequential structural decision is making Chart Canvas a shared leaf node: a single composition surface reachable from the Explore builder, from every Indicator page via an “Open in Explorer” CTA, and from every Geographic profile. When a user clicks “Open in Explorer” from the Arizona unemployment page, the chart canvas pre-loads the Arizona unemployment series alongside the national average, with no series ID lookup required. The indicator name resolves to the series ID invisibly.

Key design decisions

Plain-language search replaces series IDs

Keeping series IDs and adding a better lookup tool was rejected: it preserves the two-application problem, with users carrying a cryptic code between a lookup context and a charting context. The fix is to make the plain-language name the surface for every interaction and resolve the series ID as an implementation detail. The ID still exists (in the API reference and the permalink) but is never required as input.

Recession shading is a toggle, not a default

Shading on a 1 or 2-year chart is either misleading (no recessions in the window) or alarming (the 2020 spike dominates). So it is off by default for short ranges, easily enabled for long-range analysis. The same applies to the Fed target band and the historical average line: contextual aids that help experts but can confuse novices if always present. The toggle pattern respects both archetypes.

Demographics is a filter layer, not a section

A dedicated “Demographics” nav section was rejected for two reasons. Breakdowns are most useful in context (Black unemployment beside the national rate, not on a separate page), and a separate section implies the data is a niche interest rather than a core analytical lens. A filter chip on every Indicator page and Chart Canvas signals that disaggregated data is first-class for every indicator.

Releases is a top-level nav section

The same reasoning that elevated Track to top-level nav in the PACER redesign applies here. Release management is not a peripheral preference; it is the primary recurring workflow for Jordan, Rashida, and every professional who monitors BLS data. The 8:30 AM Eastern release on the first Friday of each month is one of the most watched moments in global financial markets.

07

Outcomes & reflection

What this project demonstrates

  • Time-series data visualization: multi-series composition, dual Y-axis, normalisation to index, contextual overlays, and revision markers.
  • Data democratisation through search design: replacing series IDs with a plain-language interface for all 6 archetypes.
  • Release management UX: calendar view, subscribe alerts, and revision history as a first-class surface unique to real-time publishing.
  • Demographic data design: breakdowns by race, gender, age, and education as a persistent filter layer rather than a separate section.
  • Economic snapshot dashboard: KPI cards with benchmark context instead of a press release feed.
  • Permalink and share architecture: serializing complex chart state in a URL, enabling sharing and embedding without an account.

What I would do next

  • Run 6 to 8 moderated usability sessions with release-day journalists, economists, and state analysts, testing the Quick versus Builder split and the search autocomplete.
  • Test the overlay toggle system: does recession shading feel discoverable, or do users conclude context is absent?
  • Build the release alert prototype and test it against Jordan’s workflow under deadline pressure.
  • Design the geographic explorer (state and metro choropleth with drill-down) as the fourth high-fidelity surface.
  • Explore a public chart gallery where users publish saved Explorer views for others to discover.
Constraints and honest limitations

This is a portfolio redesign, not a commissioned project. Personas are synthesised from secondary research rather than primary interviews with BLS users. A real engagement would require coordination with BLS’s Office of Field Operations, accessibility reviews against Section 508 requirements, and alignment with Treasury and OMB on the BLS public API.

The figures in the mockups are representative of real BLS public releases, but the prototypes are not connected to the live BLS API. Several decisions, particularly around the release alert system, would require legal review given BLS’s strict embargo policy on pre-release data.
