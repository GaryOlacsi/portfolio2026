Overview

The context

PACER provides electronic access to records from the U.S. federal courts. It is essential public infrastructure, but its interface and per-page fee model both assume professional familiarity with how the courts work.

This study reimagines search, navigation, and cost transparency for the much wider public the record actually belongs to.

The problem

Open, but not legible

The experience is built for people who already know a case number and a court. Search rewards legal fluency, records are fragmented across courts, and the per-page billing is revealed only after you have committed to a click.

Hidden structure and hidden cost are the same usability failure: in both, the system withholds what a person needs to decide what to do next. Here are some example violations from the Usability/Heuristic Audit.

Research

Who it's for, and what stops them

I ran an expert evaluation of the search and access flows, then modeled the range of people who depend on the record, from professionals to first-timers representing themselves. The throughline was a system that never orients a newcomer.

Pro se litigant

Representing myself

Goal
Find and follow their own case without a lawyer to translate.
Frustration
Terminology and court structure form a wall before the first result.

Paralegal

Working the docket

Goal
Pull filings quickly across many cases for the attorneys they support.
Frustration
Retrieval is slow, repetitive, and metered by the page.

Court reporter

Covering the courts

Goal
Monitor newsworthy filings as they appear.
Frustration
No real discovery or alerting, and the cost of browsing adds up fast.

Information architecture

The moves that matter

  1. One search across courts

    A unified, plain-language search replaces the court-by-court silos and the assumption of a known case number.

  2. Cost before the click

    Any fee is shown in context before a document is opened, so there are no surprises after the fact.

  3. A readable docket

    Filings are presented as a navigable case timeline rather than a flat, coded list.

  4. Alerts and saved searches

    People who follow cases can be told when something changes instead of paying to re-check.

Before

Current PACER

Pattern: search first, court by court, pay before you know the price.

  1. 1

    Guess the court

    Decide whether the case is district, appellate, or bankruptcy, and which district or circuit holds it.

    Which-court guesswork
  2. 2

    Sign in twice

    Authenticate to PACER, then again to that court's CM/ECF system to reach the record.

    Separate logins
  3. 3

    Pick a search path

    Choose between the national Case Locator and an individual court portal, which behave differently.

    Two systems, two behaviors
  4. 4

    Enter a court-specific case number

    Match the local number format, or fall back to a party-name search with no context.

    Format knowledge required
  5. 5

    Sift and disambiguate

    Scan results scattered across courts and case types with little to tell them apart.

    No disambiguating context
  6. 6

    Decode the docket sheet

    Read cryptic docket entry text to locate the filing you actually need.

    Legacy docket jargon
  7. 7

    Open a document, blind to cost

    Click through to a filing that is billed per page, with no running total shown first.

    No cost preview
  8. 8

    Learn the price after the fact

    See the charge only once billed; hit page caps and unexpected multi-document fees.

    Surprise billing
LoopWrong court at step 1 sends the user back to the start, often more than once.
AbandonCommon exit at step 7: the user stops at the cost wall rather than commit to an unknown charge.
After

Redesigned PACER

Pattern: one national search, plain language, price shown before you buy.

  1. 1

    Search nationally, once

    Enter parties, a case, or keywords in a single box; no court selection and one sign-in.

    No court guesswork
  2. 2

    Read unified results

    See matches from every court in one list, each tagged with court, date, and case type.

    Context on every result
  3. 3

    Open a plain-language docket

    Scan readable entry labels mapped from legacy terms, so the filing is easy to spot.

    Readable docket entries
  4. 4

    Preview the cost before buying

    Select the document and see pages, fee, and quarterly fee-waiver status ahead of purchase.

    Cost known before commit
  5. 5

    Retrieve with a running total

    Get the document; a receipt and running total stay visible so charges never surprise.

    Transparent, no dead ends

Design

The high-fidelity direction

The high-fidelity direction puts search first, surfaces cost transparently in the interface, and turns the docket into a legible timeline a non-lawyer can follow.

Type and spacing do the heavy lifting: dense legal metadata is organized so the important moments in a case are visible at a glance.

Outcome

What it argues

The redesign is a proposal. Its argument: for public infrastructure, predictability is a feature. When structure and cost are both visible up front, the record becomes usable by the public that owns it, not just the profession that navigates it daily.
