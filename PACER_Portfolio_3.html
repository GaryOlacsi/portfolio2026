01 · Heuristic Audit

11 violations across 5 user flows

A structured evaluation against Nielsen’s 10 Usability Heuristics across five core flows: account registration, case search, search results, docket view, and document retrieval and billing. Severity scored 0–4.

H1
Critical
Hidden document fees
$0.10/page incurred on click with no preview, no confirmation dialog, and no cost shown on results or docket entries.
↗ Fix
Show page count and cost on every document link. Optional confirm-before-charge. Persistent session cost indicator in header.
H2
Critical
No cross-court search
Users must select a specific court before searching. Cross-court cases require multiple separate searches.
↗ Fix
Unified search as default; court type as a filter facet, not a prerequisite. Retire the separate Case Locator.
H3
Critical
Flat, unfiltered docket list
Dockets with thousands of entries render as a single chronological table: no filtering, search, or grouping.
↗ Fix
Filter bar (type, date, party). Auto-detected “key events” panel. Collapsible routine administrative entries.
H4
Critical
Session timeout loses all context
A 15-minute timeout returns users to login with no recovery path. All filters and docket position are lost.
↗ Fix
Extend session to 60 minutes with visible countdown. Serialize all state in URL for seamless post-auth recovery.
H5
Critical
Unlinked party and judge entities
Attorney, party, and judge names are plain text. No navigation from a case to all cases involving that person or firm.
↗ Fix
Linked entity system: every name hyperlinks to a profile showing full case history. Disambiguation for name variants.
H6
Major
Mail-only registration
New accounts require mailing a form and waiting 7–10 days for postal credentials. No instant digital path.
↗ Fix
Instant email-verified activation. Mail option as fallback only. Guest preview mode for fee-waiver documents.
H7
Major
Thin search results
Results show only case number, name, court, and date. No status, judge, entry count, or fee estimate before clicking.
↗ Fix
Expand result cards: status badge, judge name, case type, entry count, last activity date, and fee estimate.
H8
Major
Billing has no analytics
A flat transaction log: no case grouping, no monthly total, no spend trend, no export, no budget alerts.
↗ Fix
Billing analytics: spend by case, timekeeper, and month. CSV export. Spending alerts. Quarterly fee-waiver progress.
H9
Major
No case tracking or alerts
No native way to watch a case for new filings. Third-party tools (CourtListener) exist specifically to fill this gap.
↗ Fix
Watch case feature with email/SMS alerts, entry-type filtering, and calendar integration for hearing dates.
H10
Minor
No mobile experience
Desktop-only design from the early 2000s. Core flows require horizontal scrolling and are unusable on mobile.
↗ Fix
Mobile-first quick lookup: case search, status and key dates, docket entry chips, single-document download with fee preview.
H11
Minor
No in-platform document viewer
Documents open as raw PDFs with no annotation, text search, citation tools, or related-document linking.
↗ Fix
Optional viewer with text search, annotation, legal citation copy, and a related-documents sidebar.
💡
Key insight from the audit
H1 (hidden fees) is the finding that most directly shapes the entire redesign. It affects 4 of 5 user archetypes at high severity and is the root of the trust problem that pervades every interaction. Every surface in the redesign incorporates a cost-transparency layer the current site completely omits.
02 · User Research & Personas

Five archetypes, one fee model

Synthesised from legal research workflow literature, journalism research methodology, public defender resource constraints, and the heuristic audit. They range from AmLaw 100 litigation associates with deep PACER expertise to first-time citizens who discover the fee model only after being charged.

Jobs to be done: Research a judge’s sentencing patterns; find precedent cases by charge type and outcome; pull all filings in a complex criminal case.

MC
Marcus Chen · Secondary
Concerned citizen, one-time user · Low legal literacy
Jobs to be done: Check if a contractor has been sued in federal court; verify a legal claim from a news article. Pain: H6, H1 · the 7–10 day registration delay blocks any access; discovers the 10¢/page fee only after being charged.
⚖️
The Natalie / Marcus design tension
Natalie (the attorney) is a power user who needs all case metadata, complex filters, and fast bulk retrieval. Marcus (the citizen) needs zero-friction access to a single case and immediate clarity on cost. These archetypes are in direct tension; the same fee transparency that protects Marcus can feel like friction for Natalie. The solution is a persistent, compact cost indicator in the header (always visible, never intrusive) rather than confirmation dialogs on every click.
03 · Information Architecture

Five intent-based sections; Track elevated to first class

The IA redesign addresses two structural failures: the requirement to pre-select a court before any search, and the absence of a case-tracking and alert system as a primary navigation surface.

Intent over court taxonomy

Search is universal by default; court type is a filter facet, not a prerequisite. Users no longer need to know the court system structure before searching.

Cost transparency everywhere

Fee estimates appear on every document link, result card, and docket entry before any click. A persistent session cost indicator lives in the global header.

Track as first-class nav

Case alerts and watched cases are elevated to a top-level nav section, not buried in account preferences, reflecting that monitoring is a primary daily workflow.

Linked entity architecture

Parties, attorneys, and judges are linked entities, not text strings. Every name navigates to a profile page, unlocking the Explore section as a new design surface.

🏛
The most structurally significant decision: elevating Track
On the current site there is no tracking feature at all; its absence is itself an H9 violation. Making it a first-class nav item signals that monitoring active cases is a primary user behavior, not a peripheral account setting. Validated by persona analysis: Natalie checks five dockets every morning, Daniel monitors litigation for his beat, and Olivia tracks public defender clients for months.

07 · Key Design Decisions

Why these choices, not others

Why the fee model is a design problem, not just a policy problem
The instinct might be to treat the $0.10/page fee as a constraint and add a “cost” column. The deeper insight: the fee model’s opacity shaped every other decision. No cost preview meant no confirm dialog; no confirm dialog meant no page counts; no page counts meant no document metadata. The H1 fix is not one change; it’s a principle (cost transparency at every surface) that cascades into 15–20 individual decisions.
Why Track is a top-level nav section, not an account feature
An early design put case alerts in Account Settings under “Notifications.” Rejected on the frequency analysis from persona research: Natalie checks dockets every morning, Daniel monitors cases for his beat, Olivia tracks clients for months. These are daily primary workflows, not preferences. Making Track a top-level section is an architectural statement that the system understands how its power users actually work.
Why the linked entity system unlocks the entire Explore section
H5 (unlinked entities) is the most structurally consequential finding because it blocks an entire section of the IA from existing. Without linked entities there are no court, judge, or party profiles; the Explore section cannot be built. Fixing H5 is not a detail-page improvement; it’s the prerequisite for the research workflows that make PACER genuinely useful for Olivia and Shreya.
Why cost transparency is woven in rather than isolated
A tempting approach is a single “cost calculator” page. Rejected because it externalizes cost from where decisions are made. A user looking at a docket entry should not navigate elsewhere to learn what downloading costs. The principle (cost transparency at the point of decision) results in fee estimates in 6 locations: results table, docket entry rows, document links, the pre-download dialog, the session-cost KPI, and the header badge.
08 · Outcomes & Reflection

What this project demonstrates

End-to-end UX practice on a high-stakes legal domain with real consequences for errors, missed deadlines, and unexpected charges. Every decision traces back to a heuristic finding or persona need.

Domain complexity

Designing for a high-stakes legal domain with real consequences for errors, missed deadlines, and unexpected charges.

Transactional UX

A cost-transparency system absent from most SaaS work; fee previews, confirm dialogs, and session tracking that balance friction vs. protection.

Court analytics dashboard

Filing volume trends, case-type breakdowns, median time to trial, and geographic distribution; a different roll-up than financial dashboards.

Linked entity architecture

Recognising that an H5 violation (plain-text names) has architectural consequences (no Explore section can exist) and designing for the dependency.

Progressive disclosure

Docket filtering, key-events detection, and collapsible entries applied to a high-volume document-retrieval context.

Alert & notification design

Watch case, entry-type filtering, and calendar integration designed as a primary UX surface rather than a buried setting.

What I would do next
  • Conduct 6–8 moderated usability sessions with attorneys, journalists, and at least one public defender researcher using the Figma prototype.
  • Test the persistent cost badge specifically: does it reassure users (the intent) or create anxiety about spending (a risk)?
  • Design the billing analytics dashboard and document viewer surfaces, the two remaining high-fidelity screens from the original scope.
  • Research the fee-waiver programme in depth; the $30 quarterly threshold is poorly understood and could be a significant trust-building lever if surfaced correctly.
  • Explore whether a “preview first page free” model is technically feasible, to address citizen and journalist use cases without eliminating the fee revenue model.
📋
Constraints and honest limitations
This is a portfolio redesign, not a commissioned project. The personas are synthesised from secondary research rather than primary interviews with PACER users. A real engagement would require stakeholder interviews with the Administrative Office of the U.S. Courts, federal judges and clerks, the legal technology community, and accessibility advocates (PACER has specific Section 508 obligations as a federal platform). Fee-model decisions would require coordination with the judiciary’s finance and technology committees.
