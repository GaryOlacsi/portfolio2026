Overview

The context

The Bureau of Labor Statistics publishes the headline indicators that move markets and shape policy: employment, inflation, and wages. The measurement is rigorous and trusted.

The delivery is where it falters. The site is organized like the bureau's org chart and presents data as tables, leaving non-experts to figure out what a number actually means. This study redesigns the path from a figure to its meaning.

The problem

Open, but not legible

Navigation mirrors how the agency is structured, not how a question is asked. Indicators arrive as dense tables with little interpretation, so a non-specialist cannot easily tell signal from noise.

The hard, careful work of measurement is undone in the last mile, at the moment of presentation. Here are some example violations from the Usability/Heuristic Audit.

Research

Who it's for, and what stops them

I ran a heuristic audit of the data discovery and reading experience, then modeled the people who need these numbers but do not work with statistics for a living. They consistently knew the figure mattered but could not tell what to do with it.

Policy analyst

Briefing the decision

Goal
Pull the right series with context for a memo, fast.
Frustration
Has to navigate by bureau and program rather than by the question.

Small-business owner

Reading the economy

Goal
Understand wage and price trends that affect the business.
Frustration
Jargon and raw tables, with no plain-language takeaway.

Economics student

Learning to read data

Goal
Find a series and interpret it correctly.
Frustration
Little guidance on what a number means or how to read it.

Information architecture

The moves that matter

  1. Organize by question

    Entry points are framed around indicators and questions ("is inflation rising?") rather than the issuing bureau.

  2. Every figure carries its meaning

    Each number is paired with a plain-language reading of what it is and how to interpret it.

  3. Trend and comparison by default

    Context over snapshots: figures are shown against their own history and related series.

  4. Definitions in place

    Terms are defined inline, at the moment they are used, not buried in a glossary.

Workflow redesign / primary task

Compare two indicators and share the result

The current site treats each economic program as its own silo reached through series IDs, and it cannot co-plot two series or hand back a shareable chart. The redesign replaces code lookup with plain-language search and routes every entry point to one Indicator Detail page where a chart is built, revisions are surfaced, and the full state lives in a link.

Scenario

Put the unemployment rate and CPI on one chart, then send it to a colleague

A common analytical task on BLS.gov: view two headline indicators together over the same window, note any revision in the latest release, and share the exact view. Today the two indicators live in separate programs (CPS and CPI) with no shared chart surface.

User

Policy analyst

Comfortable with the concepts, not with series codes. Knows what "unemployment rate" and "CPI" mean; does not know that they map to LNS14000000 and CUUR0000SA0, and should not need to.

Current state

8 steps / 2 dead ends
01
Hunt for the right program Unemployment sits under one subject area, CPI under another; no single "find an indicator" entry. Subject-first IA, not task-first
02
Pick a data tool Top Picks, One-Screen Search, Series Report, flat databases: the user must choose a tool before choosing data. Tool choice precedes intent
03
Resolve the unemployment series Select or type a series ID (LNS14000000) to retrieve the headline rate. Requires knowing a code
04
Generate a single-series chart The built-in chart plots one series with fixed options; no way to add a second indicator. Back out to add CPI
05
Repeat the whole path for CPI Return to program navigation, choose a tool again, resolve a second series ID (CUUR0000SA0). Second full pass
06
Discover the two will not co-plot Different programs render in different tools; there is no shared chart surface for both series. Task blocked in-site
07
Export both to a spreadsheet Download each series as CSV or XLSX, align the date columns by hand, build the comparison chart offline. Work leaves the site
08
Share a screenshot The colleague receives a static image; the chart is not reproducible and any revision is invisible. No shareable, live state
breaks on H1, H3, H7, H8 Visibility of system state, match to real tasks, flexibility, and recognition over recall all fail at the point the user tries to combine two indicators.

Redesigned flow

5 steps / answer-first
01
Search "unemployment" Plain-language entry from anywhere on the site; no series ID, no program to pick first. Task-first entry
02
Add to chart The name resolves to LNS14000000 invisibly and lands on the shared Indicator Detail page. Code resolved for the user
03
Add a second series Type "CPI"; it joins the same chart on a shared or dual axis without leaving the page. Native co-plotting
?
Revision in the latest release? Yes: a revision marker and note appear at the affected point. No: the series renders clean. Revisions surfaced in context

Design

The high-fidelity direction

The high-fidelity direction centers on annotated indicator views: charts that explain themselves, plain-language summaries above the detail, and comparisons that put a figure in context.

The data visualization is restrained and labeled like an instrument readout, so the eye lands on the meaning before the math.
